The Problem

Simulation can improve policies — but it can also break them.

Real-world dexterous manipulation policies are difficult to improve directly on hardware. Reinforcement learning in the real world is slow, expensive, and difficult to scale, while simulation offers safe and massively parallel interaction. However, policies optimized freely in simulation can exploit contact mismatches and dynamics discrepancies, achieving high simulated reward while failing on hardware.

The problem we investigate is off-domain policy improvement: how can we improve a real-world policy using interaction only in simulation?

⚠
Failure Mode A

Unconstrained sim RL

Freely optimizing in simulation can discover reward hacking dynamics-specific behaviors that transfer poorly to the real world.

⚠
Failure Mode B

Distributional regularization

BC constraints keep policies close to the base policy — but this can preserve slow, imprecise, or even failed behavioral modes from the original demonstrations. Too little regularization leads the exploitation of sim reward hacking behvaiors, while too much regularization prevents the improvement of the base policy.

Our approach

Support-constrained improvement

Constrain simulation optimization to behaviors already represented in the real-world policy. Improve within the support of the behavior prior, rather than matching or ignoring the full distribution.

The key insight

Policy improvement in simulation should be constrained to the support of the real world prior.

Method

Steer through the support — don’t escape it.

①
Frozen Real-World Prior

What behaviors are transferable?

A generative policy trained on real demonstrations defines the support. Only behaviors inside it can reliably transfer.

②
Support-Constrained Sim RL

Which supported behaviors are best?

RL in simulation searches within that support for higher-reward modes, guided by a privileged critic unavailable at deployment.

③
Direct Deployment

No distillation. No real-world RL.

The actor is trained directly on deployment-compatible observations — no distillation from a privileged state policy to point cloud, no sim-to-real fine-tuning stage.

Real-world base policy

Each task uses a dedicated base policy: a conditional flow matching model trained on task-specific teleoperated demonstrations. It takes a cropped scene point cloud and robot proprioception as input and produces a sequence of joint-space actions. Each base policy is kept frozen throughout all downstream training — never updated, only queried.

Simulation setup

Each task is reconstructed in simulation from CAD models with calibrated geometry and physical parameters. We use sparse binary task-completion rewards and apply domain randomization over mass, friction, object pose, and external perturbations. A privileged critic has access to ground-truth simulator state during training; the actor is restricted to the same point-cloud and proprioception observations available on the real robot.

Support-constrained steering

The steering policy modifies the base policy’s action distribution at sampling time. It is trained to maximize task reward subject to a hard support constraint: sampled actions must remain in the high-density region of the frozen prior. Actions outside the support incur a large penalty that dominates the reward signal, so the optimizer cannot trade transferability for simulated performance. At deployment the steering policy runs alongside the frozen base policy with no additional fine-tuning.

Where Unconstrained Simulation Optimization Fails

Unconstrained RL discovers strategies that are unsafe or unreliable on hardware.

Unconstrained simulation RL methods can achieve high reward in simulation while learning behavior that is erratic or dangerous on physical hardware. On soccer push, FPO pushes the hand into the table while applying force through the fingers. On lightbulb screw, RialTo applies excessive force and can pull the stand off the table. SCORE avoids these failure modes by keeping the steering policy within the support of the real-world behavior prior.

Ablations & Analysis

Understanding the components that make SCORE work.

(A & B) Data Quality

The base policy must cover the target behavior for the support constraint to be effective. More demonstration data yields higher success after sim RL (A). Workspace coverage also matters: we collect task demonstrations only on the right side of the workspace, and collect additional play data on the left side. Evaluating OOD on the left side, adding the play data substantially improves SCORE’s success while the base policy stays flat (B).

(C) Retry Behavior Improves Robustness

Including recovery and retry motions in demonstrations significantly boosts success. SCORE policies naturally leverage these modes during sim RL; the base policy alone is unaffected.

(D & E) Steering to Novel Conditions

The sim RL policy can be trained with additional context to handle conditions outside the original demonstrations. When a distractor object is present, adding distractor position as a conditioning input improves success—but performance remains limited because the base policy was never trained in this scenario, highlighting a core limitation (D). For cross-object generalization, we use the same bottle-grasp base policy and train the steering policy with a “carrot reward” to pinch an actual carrot, achieving substantial improvement over the base (E).

(F) Privileged Critics Accelerate Simulation Learning

During training we use a privileged critic with access to ground-truth simulator state (object positions, velocities) unavailable at deployment. This provides a much stronger learning signal, accelerating convergence. The actor uses only the point-cloud and proprioception observations available on the real robot.

Limitations

Behavior Prior Coverage

SCORE’s support constraint is only as broad as the task-specific base policy. If a base policy fails to cover important recovery motions or object configurations, the constraint may prevent the sim RL policy from discovering better behavior.

Sim-to-Real Gap

Despite domain randomization over mass, friction, and perturbations, residual sim-to-real gap remains. Policies that exploit simulation-specific dynamics may not transfer cleanly, and some tasks require additional randomization tuning.

Observation Fidelity

We use cropped point clouds and proprioception as observations. Sim-to-real transfer of visual observations is sensitive to sensor calibration and point cloud noise levels, which vary across robot platforms and environments.

Scaling to Broader Priors

SCORE currently trains a separate steering policy per task. Scaling to a single policy that can steer a broad multi-task prior across diverse task distributions is an open direction for future work.
